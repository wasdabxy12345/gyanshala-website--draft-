Our History & Journey

A timeline of innovation in education

The Foundation

Gyan Shala was founded by Prof. Pankaj Jain in 1999 with the aim of providing quality education to underprivileged children. Prof. Jain's vision was shaped by his experience as a member of the terminal review mission for the Andhra Pradesh Primary Education Project (APPEP) in the early 90s.

Despite substantial funding and well-designed implementation, Prof. Jain observed that the project failed to make a significant impact on students' learning outcomes, prompting him to seek a more effective educational model.

The Motivation

During his work in the mid-90s, Prof. Jain observed that girls from poor families in Bangladesh improved significantly in early schooling compared to their Indian counterparts, despite Bangladesh being economically poorer and socially less developed than India.

At the same time, India faced a critical challenge: while school enrollment was rising, nearly half of all children dropped out before completing grade five. Studies showed that most students in grades four and five could not read or perform basic arithmetic—a trend that threatened to leave half of adult India functionally illiterate by 2030.

These experiences posed a profound intellectual challenge. It led Prof. Jain to realize that traditional schooling needed a completely different approach to succeed at scale, eventually leading him to initiate the research that would define the Gyan Shala model.

Need of a New Model

Prof. Jain spent four years studying various programs like AMUL and BRAC. He learned that traditional model of a team of teachers headed by a principal only served the top 5% of the population effectively.

Furthermore, with government spending per student being high, universal coverage under the existing system would cost upwards of INR 3,600 billion—well beyond India's budgetary capacity. We needed a model that delivers high-quality learning outcomes at a mass scale, while keeping costs low and leveraging available resources.

Genesis & Growth

From all his learnings and experiences, Prof. Jain, along with some of his colleagues at IIM Ahmedabad and IRMA, initiated the Gyan Shala program under the Education Support Organization (ESO) in 2000.

Gyan Shala began with just 10 1st-grade classes in Ahmedabad. By 2015-16, Gyan Shala then expanded to 1,270 classes, reaching around 35,000 children across 9 cities in 4 states.

Gyan Shala's work continues as they test the viability of their model in rural settings and partner with state governments in Gujarat and Bihar to improve school quality.

Our Approach

The system of education that Prof. Jain developed have four distinct features:

Child-Centric Focus

More focus on children's capability to learn rather than on enhancing the teacher's capability to teach.

Team-Based Teaching

Reengineering the class's teacher's role into a team effort, six-tiered team, to bring curriculum designing close to the classroom, and demystifying the teacher's role.

System Organization

Take an overall system organization perspective to design a school program, instead of just focusing on only teaching, learning, and curriculum processes.

Independent Assessment

Periodic assessments by reputed external agencies (like EI using ASSET tests) to gauge effectiveness and drive continuous program improvement.

Ensuring Regularity

We ensure students stay in school by locating classes near homes, maintaining a positive environment free of physical punishment, and providing all learning materials free of charge.

Teacher Support

Teachers receive extensive Program-specific training and supervisory support visits twice a week, ensuring quality remains consistent across all classes.

The 5A Implementation

Complementing our academic features, the 5A approach ensures we overcome socio-economic barriers to reach every child:

Awareness

Behavioral Change towards education through continuous community engagement and motivation.

Availability

Ensuring quality schooling is present in every slum cluster, making education available to all marginalized children.

Accessibility

Equitable residential education held within the community (walking distance) with suitable timings for students.

Affordability

No indirect costs (books, transport, stationeries), removing barriers for poor families.

Accountability

Daily evaluations and external assessments guided by dedicated MEL staff for course correction.

We learn from various education experts and best practices from across the world, and ensure implementation through our 1,200-person team, which includes teachers, field supervisors, and curriculum designer-cum-managers. Despite being conducted in slum areas, our classrooms are equipped with all the necessary facilities and follow best classroom practices such as whole class teaching, small group activities for peer learning, and self-learning through daily workbooks.
