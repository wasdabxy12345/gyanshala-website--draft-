About Gyan Shala

Dedicated to providing quality education to underprivileged children since 1999.

Our History & Journey

Gyan Shala was founded by Prof. Pankaj Jain, alongside colleagues from Indian Institute of Management, Ahmedabad (IIMA) and Institute of Rural Management, Anand (IRMA), to develop and implement a highly effective and quality education model for India's underserved children at scale.

The initiation was sparked by Prof. Jain's experience on the terminal review mission for the Andhra Pradesh Primary Education Project (APPEP) in the early 90s. Despite being well-funded and professionally executed, the British-backed program failed to produce significant gains in student learning outcomes. During his other work, he observed that Bangladeshi girls from poor families in Bangladesh were outperforming their Indian counterparts in early schooling, despite Bangladesh being economically poorer and socially more conservative at the time. These observations challenged his fundamental understanding of pedagogy and led him on a quest.

Throughout the mid-to-late 90s, he studied various government and non-government school programs in India and many other countries. His research led to a critical insight that the traditional model of quality schooling, which relied heavily on exceptional teachers led by exceptional principals or leaders, could only serve the top 5% of children. But for the remaining 95%, a new approach was needed to evolve that could deliver effective, high-quality education on a mass scale using readily available and sustainable human and financial resources. To bridge this gap, he wanted to evolve and implement a pedagogical model inspired by various excellent schooling systems and the world's leading large-scale development programs like AMUL and BRAC. To implement this vision, Prof. Jain and his colleagues founded Gyan Shala under the Education Support Organization.

They began with just 10 1st-grade classes in Ahmedabad in 1999. By 2015-16, Gyan Shala then expanded to 1,270 classes, reaching around 35,000 children across 9 cities in 4 states.

Our work continues as we test the viability of our model in rural settings and partner with state governments in Gujarat and Bihar to improve government school outcomes.

Our Approach

The system of education that Prof. Jain developed have four distinct features:

Child-Centric Focus

More focus on children's capability to learn rather than on enhancing the teacher's capability to teach.

Team-Based Teaching

Reengineering the class's teacher's role into a team effort, four-tiered team, to bring curriculum designing close to the classroom, and demystifying the teacher's role.

System Organization

Take an overall system organization perspective to design a school program, instead of just focusing on only teaching, learning, and curriculum processes.

Independent Assessment

Periodic assessments by reputed external agencies (like EI using ASSET tests) to gauge effectiveness and drive continuous program improvement.

Ensuring Regularity

We ensure students stay in school by locating classes near homes, maintaining a positive environment free of physical punishment, and providing all learning materials free of charge.

Teacher Support

Teachers receive extensive Program-specific training and supervisory support visits twice a week, ensuring quality remains consistent across all classes.

The 5A Implementation

Complementing our academic features, the 5A approach ensures we overcome socio-economic barriers to reach every child:

Awareness

Behavioral Change towards education through continuous community engagement and motivation.

Availability

Ensuring quality schooling is present in every slum cluster, making education available to all marginalized children.

Accessibility

Equitable residential education held within the community (walking distance) with suitable timings for students.

Affordability

No indirect costs (books, transport, stationeries), removing barriers for poor families.

Accountability

Daily evaluations and external assessments guided by dedicated MEL staff for course correction.

We learn from various education experts and best practices from across the world, and ensure implementation through our 1,200-person team, which includes teachers, field supervisors, and curriculum designer-cum-managers. Despite being conducted in slum areas, our classrooms are equipped with all the necessary facilities and follow best classroom practices such as whole class teaching, small group activities for peer learning, and self-learning through daily workbooks.

Our Team

To deliver high-quality education at a mass scale, Gyan Shala operates through a specialized six-tier team structure.

This model deconstructs the traditional role of a single teacher into a team-based effort, ensuring that curriculum design, field support, and classroom delivery work in perfect harmony.

CEO
Coordinate with all Team Leads to manage cross-city programs, handle donor relations, fundraise, and ensure the long-term sustainability of the organization.
Team Leaders
Instruct and coordinate the curriculum design teams, evaluate study materials, and serve as the vital link between academic operations and executive leadership.
Curriculum Designers
Architect the student workbooks and teacher guides. They transform high-level goals into daily lessons and iterate materials based on field feedback.
Senior Supervisors
Lead monthly supervisor meetings, prepare staff for teacher training, and bridge the gap between the Curriculum team and field implementation.Oversee the work of supervisors, conduct regular field visits, and ensure the quality of instruction and student outcomes across multiple centers.
Supervisors
Provide on-ground support to teachers in conducting classes, assist with community fieldwork, and attend monthly alignment meetings to maintain instructional quality.
Class Teachers
Gyan Shala replaces the traditional single-teacher model with a robust "Chain of Support." This ensures that our 1,200+ frontline educators are backed by layers of academic, supervisory, and strategic expertise.
